Free TextEXAM: MRI NECK WITH AND WITHOUT CONTRASTCLINICAL HISTORY: Male, 45 years of age, presenting with a history of persistent hoarseness and suspicion of laryngeal pathology. COMPARISON: None. TECHNIQUE: MRI of the neck was performed using pre- and post-contrast T1-weighted, T2-weighted, and STIR sequences in axial, coronal, and sagittal planes. Post-contrast imaging was obtained following intravenous administration of 9 mL of Gadavist. FINDINGS: The nasopharynx, oropharynx, and hypopharynx appear unremarkable without any evidence of mass or abnormal enhancement. The larynx demonstrates thickening and irregular enhancement involving the left true vocal cord, extending from the anterior commissure to the arytenoid cartilage. The lesion measures approximately 20 x 8 x 6 mm. There is no evidence of cartilage invasion or extension into the paraglottic space. Paranasal sinuses reveal mild mucosal thickening in the maxillary sinuses bilaterally, without opacification. The mastoid air cells and middle ear cavities are clear. The cervical spine is unremarkable, without fracture or malalignment. The lung apices are clear. Salivary glands and thyroid gland appear normal. No significant cervical lymphadenopathy is identified. The largest lymph node, at the left level III, measures 9 x 5 mm. The arteries and veins of the neck are unremarkable. IMPRESSION: Thickening and irregular enhancement involving the left true vocal cord, measuring approximately 20 x 8 x 6 mm, suspicious for laryngeal neoplasm. Direct laryngoscopy and biopsy are recommended for further evaluation. Mild mucosal thickening in bilateral maxillary sinuses, likely inflammatory. No significant cervical lymphadenopathy or vascular abnormalities.

TECHNIQUE: CT neck was performed following administration of 35 mL IV contrast. The estimated cumulative dose (DLP [mGy-cm]) is 209.FINDINGS: The soft tissues of the neck appear unremarkable. The nasopharynx, oropharynx, hypopharynx, and trachea show no abnormalities, aside from right vocal cord paralysis. There is mild scattered mucosal thickening in the paranasal sinuses and small bilateral mastoid effusions. The major salivary glands appear normal. However, infiltration of the right thyroid lobe by adjacent adenopathy is noted. Significant lymphadenopathy is observed in the neck, including a conglomerate right level III/IV and supraclavicular lymph node on series 301, image 40, measuring at least 4.4 x 3.7 cm. This has enlarged since the prior exam. On the left side, a level III/IV and supraclavicular lymph node conglomerate is seen on series 301, image 49, measuring at least 3.8 x 3.6 cm. Additionally, prominent and clustered left level V lymph nodes are visible on series 301, image 61. The right lower cervical/supraclavicular conglomerate nodal mass compresses or involves the right internal jugular vein and encases the proximal right common carotid artery and right vertebral artery. Mild multilevel degenerative changes are present in the cervical spine. The patient has a history of left posterior occipital craniotomy, and ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament is observed at C3-4-5-6. The lung apices reveal no significant abnormalities, and the right paratracheal lymphadenopathy infiltrates the inferior aspect of the right thyroid lobe. Further evaluation of intrathoracic findings should be done with a dedicated CT chest examination. The visualized brain parenchyma shows evidence of status post left posterior parietal craniotomy without enhancement in the underlying brain. IMPRESSION: Bilateral cervical and supraclavicular lymphadenopathy showing progression of nodal metastatic disease. Compression and possible invasion of the right internal jugular vein by right cervical adenopathy, encasement of the proximal right common carotid artery, and proximal right vertebral artery. Increasing infiltration of the right inferior thyroid lobe by adjacent adenopathy.
